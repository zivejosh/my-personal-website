### What Are We Dealing With Here?

Now, when I say "The Dangers of Social Media," I'm probably not referring to what you're used to. When we talk about "online safety," most people immediately picture viruses, hackers, and phishing scams. However, in this context, I am going to completely break down my personal gripes and burdens with something that I believe is (subtly and potentially) way worse.

I want to preface this: Social media, on a completely surface level, is probably one of the most incredible and innovative ideas to ever come out of the internet. Through this marvel of technology, we've been able to achieve vast worldwide communication, create public town squares, stay constantly connected to our peers, reach much further into marketing endeavours, and the list goes on and on. However, this article is going to deal with the dangers of those positives, what they have done to me, and by proxy, what they are probably doing to you.

I'm sure you've heard it before: "It's those darn screens." But really, it _is_ "those darn screens." We as a society have not been granted a gift that simply benefits us through mass communication. Rather, we have been bestowed a medium for mass communication that profits off our usage. It's through this exact philosophy that we have become slaves to our screens, finding it impossible to detach ourselves from the chains of social media.

### What Do They Have To Gain?

Before we get into the inner ideas of why personal usage of social software is generally a net negative, it's important to talk about why the software itself is inherently flawed. That conversation starts with a simple question: "What do these big tech bros have to gain?" The answer is simple: **MONEY!!!**

What a shock, am I right? Trillion-dollar companies want more money. How do they get this money? Well, it's a known fact they basically get it through ads. Companies like Instagram, YouTube, TikTok, etc., all make money by selling ad space on their platforms to companies that want to sell things to YOU.

I remember when I first heard of this idea. In my mind, I was like, "Yeah, no doi." Plus, I genuinely thought that was probably the best way for a company like this to maintain itself and make a consistent amount of money. But just because something makes sense does not mean it shouldn't require further questioning. So, my first question on the matter was simple:

"Why is this ad space even valuable to begin with?"

To put it simply, the fact that popular social media sites garner heavy viewership creates a platform where companies feel it's worth it to advertise. It's similar to how, when cable television was the dominant medium to reach people, companies would advertise there to obtain a larger audience.

However, this is another "no doi" argument. What people (and by people, I mean me) failed to understand was that once we had a market of social media companies co-existing, it created fierce competition i.e., which platform could garner the most ad revenue and thereby make the most money?

Therefore, instead of platforms simply using ads to obtain money, we now have a much deeper issue on our hands. We have a competition over which platform can get companies to buy the most ads. That means these platforms need to garner the most viewership in order to fight for a viable space on the internet.

If money is the goal, viewership is the game.

### Viewership and Software Development

Once we recognize that these companies require strong viewership to maintain ad revenue and a strong internet presence, a stark realization becomes apparent. If a company wants to make money, they need to keep you on their platform for as long as humanly possible to squeeze as much money out of advertisers as they can. In short: **YOU** are the product, and **YOU** are being sold to companies who want your attention.

This first became readily apparent to me around late 2025. Instagram's UI used to be very simple. Reels and my feed were on the left side of the navigation bar, and my regular feed and DMs were on the right side. It was smooth since everything could be managed through swiping (thanks, Instagram!). However, Meta decided to make a VERY small change to their software. Instead of having the feed and reels on the left side of the nav bar (which categorically made sense to group together), Meta updated it so that reels would sit right in between the basic feed and your DMs. After being acclimated to Instagram's UI flow for years, I now found myself mindlessly scrolling reels when all I wanted was to see what my friends had DM'd me.

This update enraged me beyond compare. It was like opening Pandora's box. I already knew that reels and the "TikTok" format were bad for me. But I didn't care because it felt like a choice. I wanted to use reels, so I used reels. Now, however, it felt glaringly obvious that Instagram was baiting me. They were forcing me into their addictive loop for as long as they could keep me there.

Then I started noticing it everywhere. Reels sent by a friend would continually swipe infinitely; my feed was 90% reels keeping me in the loop; even my personal feed was getting recommendations from accounts I didn't follow, just to keep me engaged. Essentially, I began to realize how these social media companies effectively design their entire software to keep users glued to their screens for as long as humanly possible. That fact doesn't just apply to the format they deliver content through; it pertains to the very nature of the software itself how they get you hooked into that addictive loop to begin with.

### My Initial Solution

My initial solution was to remove the "Addictive Loop" altogether. I had this dream where I could imagine Instagram or YouTube, but with no reels. Where I could rid myself of the loop and only communicate with friends or watch long-form content. I was certain this was the answer.

So, I made the glaring decision to switch to an Android phone. I know for you iPhone users out there, that is very scary. But I did it with the desire to have more control over my system. I found an app called "NoScroll," which did exactly what I had dreamed of: it removed access to all short-form content (TikToks, Reels, Shorts) from every social media app and site. In my mind, I was a free man.

And honestly, it worked. My screen time went down, my productivity went up, I found I could focus more on longer tasks, and I still maintained the connection to my friends that I wasn't willing to part with.

### The Reality

Don't get me wrong, this did help a lot. Everything I mentioned above still holds VERY true, and anyone who wants to try that method definitely should, because it got me off the most addictive part of the internet.

But the reality I had to face afterward was that even though I removed the most addictive part of social media, I didn't remove my addiction _to_ social media. I still found myself mindlessly scrolling through feeds, coming up with loopholes to get my fix of funny short videos. There was always a workaround (especially through Instagram). Soon I came to understand that the addiction itself wasn't just to short-form content, but to the social media platforms themselves. The UI and design of the whole software were built to keep you hooked; if it wasn't one thing, it was another.

### Additional Negatives

However, this corporate greed and obvious addiction-fishing wasn't enough to kick me off the platforms just yet. It took months of analysis and coming to terms with how these platforms worked before I decided to rid myself of them entirely.

It actually started with misinformation. I've always been one to question a source or a stat. I've never been one to hear something and immediately take it as fact. I need proof, and I need to believe that the proof comes from a credible source. However, I began to realize that the way my mind was adapting to current events was being heavily influenced by social media.

The algorithms on these platforms aren't built to benefit you at all. Rather, they are meant to bolster posts that can garner the most viewership. To these companies, that means pushing whatever will get everyone looking. Companies like Twitter THRIVE on this. Essentially, if just as many people are outraged by something as they are loving it, the post gets pushed up the algorithm. This creates extreme polarization in the public town square.

Because posts that bolster obvious love and obvious hate are pushed everywhere in an addictive, short (and misleading) manner, people begin to drift towards one extreme or the other. What does this mean? Our view of the world and the lens we see it through is automatically tampered with and skewed. We are letting a machine decide what constitutes the reality of our world when we should be the ones dictating that. Algorithms bolster both the immense horrors and the exuberant greatness of our world, leading us to believe either that the world is falling apart or that the world is perfect. Neither is true. The truth is, the world is far more boring and way more realistic than what social media portrays.

Which brings me to my next point.

This doesn't just pertain to the world at large, but also to our personal lives. This algorithm-driven effect has warped how we want to be perceived on social media. Everyone wants to have a life; everyone wants to look successful and happy. Because the algorithm pushes it, we feel the need to push it as well. Social media has become our dystopian medium for this.

The fact of the matter is that everyone is normal. Sometimes we're going through bad spots, sometimes good, sometimes amazing, and sometimes we hit rock bottom. What I find despicable is the feeling that we _must_ share our perceived greatness with the world, no matter how horrid the truth of our lives actually is. I know for a fact that we, as a people, are not naturally that self-absorbed.

So, I made a decision. My life didn't need to be on display for everyone to see. The life that was on display wasn't honest anyway. It was a persona I created so the people around me could... honestly, I don't even know, "get my vibe," I guess? I had over 1,000 followers on Instagram, but I don't even think I speak to 1,000 people in a whole year. So why did I need 1,000 people seeing a carefully curated portrayal of me 24/7? Especially when the medium itself was warping my reality.

With that, I deleted social media. I got rid of the addiction altogether. I couldn't stand the self-absorption, the fakeness, the algorithms, and the corporate greed. I chose to have a choice in what I put in front of my eyes. I chose to not let a tech company decide what I wanted.

### So Now What?

The few of you reading this vent of an article might be thinking, "Wow, so what do you do now that you've gotten rid of social media?"

Well, to keep in contact with people, I do this ancient thing called "texting them." I came to the conclusion that if I really wanted to keep someone updated on my life, I would just tell them. I found no reason to have it on public display. Plus, doesn't it sound way more real to actually talk with someone about your endeavors rather than just having them infer your life is great from a post?

This whole concept (albeit very rudimentary) has opened me up emotionally. I'm not afraid to talk about what's going on with me. I don't feel obligated to hide behind a social media mask anymore, nor do I feel tied to my phone in a way that felt obligatory before.

I've freed up around 4 hours daily on my phone. Without mindlessly scrolling, I read more, I learn more, I love more, and I feel more. I don't feel like the world is constantly crashing down on me, because I'm no longer being pushed to believe it is. I have optimism and hope and with social media, that wasn't something I used to have.

So anyway, do what you want with what I've said here today. I've laid out my feelings on the subject in its entirety. My contact info is above if you want to discuss this further, but I implore you to really think about what I've said.

Social Media is evil, and removing it changed my life.
